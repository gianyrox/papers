## SECTION 3: PHILOSOPHICAL AND THEORETICAL GROUNDING

The article's thesis is that stablecoins represent "the most significant financial infrastructure upgrade since electronic banking." This needs intellectual foundations beyond assertion. Research the following:

### A. Monetary Theory
- **Friedrich Hayek — "Denationalisation of Money" (1976)**: his argument for competing private currencies. How do stablecoins fulfill (or not) Hayek's vision? Any modern economists applying Hayek's framework to stablecoins?
- **The "new monetary theory" of stablecoins**: any academic papers arguing stablecoins represent a new monetary form (not exactly fiat, not crypto, something new)?
- **Gresham's Law**: "bad money drives out good" — but in stablecoin adoption, GOOD money (stable dollars) drives out BAD money (inflating local currencies). Is there academic discussion of this inversion?
- **The velocity of money**: how does stablecoin adoption affect monetary velocity in countries where it's significant? Any economic modeling?
- **Seigniorage theory**: the redistribution of seigniorage from governments to private issuers (Tether). Academic treatment?

### B. Network Economics
- **Metcalfe's Law**: the value of a network is proportional to the square of the number of users. How does this apply to stablecoin adoption? Any papers modeling stablecoin network effects?
- **Platform economics / two-sided markets**: stablecoins as a platform connecting senders and receivers, borrowers and lenders. Academic framework?
- **Tipping point theory**: at what adoption threshold does stablecoin use become self-reinforcing? Is there a mathematical model?
- **Lock-in and switching costs**: once a population adopts USD stablecoins, how hard is it to switch to an alternative (local CBDC, non-USD stablecoin)?

### C. The "New Ledger" Framework
This is the article's core philosophical claim: "Money is not new. The ledger is new. Putting fiat on a shared, programmable, global ledger is what changes everything."

- Is there academic support for the idea that the LEDGER (not the currency) is the revolutionary element?
- Historical parallels: double-entry bookkeeping (1494, Luca Pacioli) revolutionized commerce. The telegraph revolutionized financial communication. SWIFT created a shared messaging layer. Are there published works connecting stablecoins to this lineage of ledger/infrastructure innovations?
- **The containerization parallel**: the AIER article comparing stablecoins to shipping containers is powerful. Are there OTHER published analyses making similar infrastructure-standardization arguments?
- **The distinction between money and payment rails**: any economists who argue we should think about stablecoins as INFRASTRUCTURE rather than CURRENCY?

### D. Identity and Privacy Theory
The article claims "identity becomes optional" with stablecoins — identity reattaches contextually rather than being embedded in the financial system.

- **Self-sovereign identity (SSI)**: academic and industry papers on SSI and its intersection with stablecoins/crypto
- **The right to financial privacy**: any legal scholarship on whether financial privacy is a human right? How does this connect to stablecoin design?
- **Surveillance capitalism**: does the stablecoin paradigm reduce surveillance (vs banks) or increase it (public ledgers)?
- **Verifiable credentials and zero-knowledge proofs**: academic papers on the feasibility of "compliance without surveillance"

### E. Inequality and Development Economics
- **Financial inclusion literature**: what does the academic evidence say about whether digital money (mobile money, crypto) actually reduces poverty? Any RCTs or rigorous studies?
- **The M-Pesa evidence**: what did controlled studies show about M-Pesa's impact on poverty in Kenya? Can this be extended to stablecoins?
- **Digital divide**: academic work on whether digital financial tools help the poorest or primarily benefit those already somewhat connected
- **The "leapfrog" hypothesis**: developing countries skipping traditional banking and going straight to stablecoin-based finance, like they skipped landlines for mobile. Any academic support?
